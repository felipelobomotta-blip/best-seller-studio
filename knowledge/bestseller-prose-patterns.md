# Bestseller Prose & Craft Patterns: Data-Driven Research

> Compiled 2026-03-13. Sources: Ben Blatt's *Nabokov's Favorite Word Is Mauve* (2017), Archer & Jockers' *The Bestseller Code* (2016), computational linguistics studies, Language Log, Readable.com, and publishing industry data.

---

## 1. Readability Metrics

### Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level

| Category | Grade Level |
|---|---|
| Average adult reading ability | 9th grade |
| Recreational reading preference | 7th grade |
| Bestsellers (1960s average) | 8.0 |
| Bestsellers (2010s average) | 6.0 |
| Most published fiction | 4-6 |
| James Patterson (bestselling fiction author of the century) | 5th grade |

**The trend is clear: bestseller reading level dropped 2 full grades in 50 years (8th to 6th).**

Out of #1 NYT bestsellers since the 1960s, 28 had a grade level below 5th grade -- and 26 of those 28 were written after 2000.

### Specific Bestseller Examples

| Book | FK Grade Level |
|---|---|
| The Grapes of Wrath (Steinbeck) | 4.1 |
| To Kill a Mockingbird (Lee) | 4.6 |
| The Great Gatsby (Fitzgerald) | 5.5 |
| 1984 (Orwell) | 8.9 |

### Sentence Length

- Target average: **14-20 words per sentence**
- Thrillers and romance: shorter sentences dominate
- Only ~1% of text should contain words over 4 syllables (Fitzgerald benchmark)
- Sentences rarely exceed 25-30 words in commercial fiction
- Variation in sentence length is key -- monotone length kills pace

### Takeaway for Your Manuscript

Simpler is not dumber. The most commercially successful fiction reads at 6th-7th grade level. Accessibility is a feature, not a bug. The reader's brain should process the *story*, not decode the *syntax*.

## 2. Dialogue Patterns

### Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

| Type | Dialogue % |
|---|---|
| Average fiction | ~30% |
| Commercial bestsellers (Cornwell, Kellerman, Cussler sample) | 43-59% (mean: 51%) |
| Genre fiction general range | 25-50% |
| Sherlock Holmes (Conan Doyle) | 47% |
| Dracula (Stoker) | 47.5% |
| The Man Who Was Thursday (Chesterton) | 60% |
| To the Lighthouse (Woolf) | 3.3% |
| The Old Man and the Sea (Hemingway) | 15% |

**Commercial fiction runs 30-50% dialogue. Literary fiction varies wildly.**

### "Said" vs. Creative Tags (Ben Blatt Data)

Blatt categorized dialogue tags into three bands:
- **Quiet**: whispered, murmured
- **Neutral**: said, asked
- **Loud**: exclaimed, shouted

**The consensus across all data**: the best-regarded and best-selling authors overwhelmingly default to "said" and "asked." Creative tags ("exclaimed," "interjected," "gasped") correlate with weaker prose.

Elmore Leonard's Rule 3: "Never use a verb other than 'said' to carry dialogue." His data backs the rule -- Leonard's prose is among the cleanest by every metric Blatt measured.

### Attribution Best Practice

- Use "said" as the invisible default
- Action beats > dialogue tags when possible
- Creative tags ("she hissed," "he ejaculated") signal amateur writing in data
- Untagged dialogue (when speakers are clear) is the mark of confident, skilled prose

## 3. Adverb Usage (Blatt Data)

### -ly Adverbs per 10,000 Words

| Author | -ly Adverbs/10k Words |
|---|---|
| Toni Morrison | 76 |
| Ernest Hemingway | 80 |
| Mark Twain | 81 |
| John Steinbeck | 93 |
| John Updike | 102 |
| Stephen King | 105 |
| E.L. James (Fifty Shades) | 155 |

**Morrison is more Hemingway than Hemingway.** She has stated: "I never say 'She says softly.'" She creates space around words so readers interpret tone without adverbial modifiers.

### The Pattern

- Great authors: 76-105 adverbs per 10,000 words
- Weaker commercial fiction: 130-155+ per 10,000 words
- The "never use adverbs" advice holds up in the data: authors who preach it mostly practice it
- Fewer adverbs = more trust in the reader's intelligence

## 4. Exclamation Marks & Cliches (Blatt Data)

### Exclamation Marks per 100,000 Words

| Author | Exclamation Marks/100k Words |
|---|---|
| James Joyce | 1,105 |
| Tom Wolfe | 929 |
| Sinclair Lewis | 844 |
| E.B. White | 782 |
| J.R.R. Tolkien | 767 |
| Elmore Leonard | 49 |

Leonard's rule: "You are allowed no more than two or three [exclamation marks] per 100,000 words of prose." He practiced what he preached.

### Cliches per 100,000 Words

- **James Patterson**: 160 cliches per 100,000 words (highest measured)
- Patterson is the most commercially successful fiction author of the 21st century AND the most cliched
- Gender split: the most cliche-heavy authors are all men
- This creates an interesting tension: cliches may actually *help* commercial pacing (recognition speed) even if they hurt literary quality

## 5. Opening Patterns (First Page / First Chapter)

### What the Data Shows (Analysis of 100 Bestseller Openings)

- **Verbs dominate over adjectives** in opening lines -- bestsellers open with action, not decoration
- **Place references are common**: home, cities, streets, cars -- concrete spatial anchoring
- **Simple colors**: black, white -- not elaborate visual poetry
- **Character introduction through action**: not through static description

### First Sentence Types (Ranked by Frequency in Bestsellers)

1. **Statement of fact/situation** -- most common
2. **Action in progress** -- close second
3. **Character introduction via voice/thought**
4. **Dialogue** -- less common than expected
5. **Question** -- rare in bestsellers
6. **Pure description/setting** -- least effective opener

### Inciting Incident Placement

- **Commercial fiction**: ideally first page, certainly first chapter
- **Literary/book club fiction**: can afford to establish status quo first, but still within first 3 chapters
- **Agent test window**: first 3-5 pages determine whether they keep reading
- **Standard agent request**: first 3 chapters or 10,000 words -- inciting incident should land within this window

### What Agents Look For in First 5 Pages

1. **Voice** -- is this writer unique?
2. **Conflict signal** -- tension, not just situation
3. **Character we want to follow** -- desire + obstacle visible immediately
4. **Pacing** -- no throat-clearing, no info-dump
5. **Writing quality** -- sentence-level competence

## 6. Sensory Detail Frequency

### Distribution of Senses in Fiction (Academic Study: 8,000+ Books)

| Sense | Frequency Rank | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Visual (sight) | 1st (dominant) | Most common by far |
| Auditory (hearing) | 2nd | Second most included |
| Tactile (touch) | 3rd | Often underdeveloped |
| Olfactory (smell) | 4th | Most evocative when used; smell-smell clusters appear frequently |
| Gustatory (taste) | 5th (least) | Rarest in fiction |

### Cross-Sense Clustering Patterns

- Smell descriptors cluster together (smell begets more smell)
- Hearing-smell and hearing-touch pair frequently
- Hearing-sight, sight-taste, and sight-touch do NOT naturally cluster
- Underused senses (smell, touch, taste) create stronger immersion when deployed because they break expectation

### Concrete vs. Abstract Language

From The Bestseller Code: bestselling authors convey meaning primarily through **nouns and verbs** (concrete) rather than adjectives and adverbs (abstract). The reading experience is smoother and more fluid when built on concrete language.

## 7. POV and Narrative Distance

### First Person vs. Third Person by Genre

| Genre | Dominant POV |
|---|---|
| Thrillers / Crime | Third person |
| Literary fiction | Third person (but first person rising) |
| Romance (contemporary) | Third person (first person rising fast) |
| Romance (historical) | Third person |
| YA | First person (dominant since 2010s) |
| New Adult | First person |
| Erotic romance | First person |
| Sci-Fi / Fantasy | Third person |
| Mystery | Third person (classic) |
| Memoir / Autofiction | First person (inherently) |
| Middle Grade | Split ~55/45 first/third |

### Deep POV Trend (2020s)

Deep third-person POV has become the dominant technique in contemporary commercial fiction. It:
- Removes visible narrator voice
- Writes AS the character (not about them)
- Eliminates filter words ("she felt," "he saw," "she thought")
- Creates intimacy without first-person limitations
- Is what "modern readers are looking for" per industry consensus

### Unreliable Narrator

No hard percentage data exists, but unreliable narrators dominate recent psychological thriller bestsellers:
- Gone Girl (Flynn) -- cultural phenomenon
- The Girl on the Train (Hawkins) -- massive seller
- The Silent Patient (Michaelides) -- 65M+ copies

The technique is strongly associated with the psychological thriller genre and has become almost expected there.

## 8. Chapter Structure

### Average Chapter Length by Genre

| Genre / Example | Words/Chapter |
|---|---|
| General fiction average | 2,000-5,000 |
| Masterworks average | ~3,345 |
| Literary fiction (Middlesex) | ~6,023 |
| Fantasy (Game of Thrones) | ~5,000 |
| Thriller (Patterson) | often under 1,000 |
| Thriller (Gone Girl) | ~2,270 |
| YA (Hunger Games) | ~3,600 |

### Short Chapters = Faster Pace

James Patterson's signature is ultra-short chapters (often 2-3 pages). He is the bestselling fiction author of this century. Dan Brown uses the same technique. The pattern:
- Short chapters create a "just one more chapter" compulsion
- Each chapter end is a decision point -- short chapters reduce friction
- Patterson and Brown alternate POVs across short chapters, creating structural cliffhangers

### Chapter Ending Patterns

Three dominant types:
1. **Cliffhanger** -- action interrupted mid-climax (thrillers)
2. **Question/revelation** -- new information that demands the next chapter
3. **Emotional shift** -- character reaches a new emotional state, reader needs resolution

Dan Brown: "Cliffhangers are about withholding and timing. It's about not telling somebody something... and creating a break before a resolution."

Best practice: **alternate** cliffhanger endings with natural-pause endings. All cliffhangers = exhausting. All pauses = boring.

### Epigraphs

- Growing trend: more authors using epigraphs in 2020s
- Can appear at book level (front matter) or per chapter
- Most common source: literature/poetry quotes, religious texts
- Function: set mood/tone, signal theme, create intellectual framework
- No hard data on correlation with commercial success

## 9. The Bestseller Code (Archer & Jockers, Machine Learning Analysis)

### Methodology

- Analyzed 20,000+ novels (refined to ~5,000 for core study)
- 2,800 data points per novel
- Algorithm predicts NYT bestseller status with **80-90% accuracy**

### Key Findings

**Topics That Sell:**
- Human closeness / intimate relationships
- Technology
- Work / career
- Family life
- Marriage, death, legal matters
- Schools, kids, moms

**Topics That DON'T Sell:**
- Explicit sex (bestsellers have LESS sex than non-bestsellers)
- Drugs, seduction, body descriptions
- Philosophical/existential tangents
- Dinner parties, card games, dancing
- Over-dressed characters
- "Big emotions" (passionate love, desperate grief) -- paradoxically, restraint sells

**Emotional Arc:**
- All novels fall into ~7 fundamental plot shapes
- Specific shape matters less than **rhythm** -- consistent, symmetrical beat of highs and lows
- Da Vinci Code and Fifty Shades share remarkably similar emotional rhythms
- Emotional pacing symmetry is a strong predictor

**Character Verbs:**
- Top 4 verbs for bestselling characters: **need, want, miss, love**
- Bestselling characters "need" and "want" 2x more often than non-bestsellers
- "Miss" and "love" appear 1.5x more often
- Active protagonists outsell passive ones

**Style:**
- Everyday vernacular > formal/literary prose
- Nouns and verbs > adjectives and adverbs
- Readers prefer prose that doesn't call attention to itself

**Setting:**
- Urban settings preferred
- Specific city doesn't matter (NYC vs Chicago vs anywhere)
- Setting is one of the weakest predictors of success

## 10. Show vs. Tell Ratio

No rigorous quantitative studies exist measuring exact show/tell ratios across bestsellers. However, the available guidance converges:

### What the Data Suggests

- Inner thoughts (internalization) are the "heartbeat" of successful fiction
- The four narrative elements must be balanced: **dialogue, action, description, inner thoughts**
- Rule of thumb: don't use the same type of sentence three times in a row
- Literature's advantage over film is interiority -- readers come to novels specifically for access to characters' inner worlds

### Practical Pattern

Bestselling prose cycles rapidly between:
1. Action/movement
2. Dialogue
3. Internal reaction/thought
4. Sensory observation

The ratio shifts by scene type (action scenes = less internalization, emotional scenes = more), but the cycling itself is constant.

## Summary: The Measurable Bestseller Prose Profile

| Metric | Bestseller Standard |
|---|---|
| Reading level | 6th-7th grade FK |
| Sentence length | 14-20 words average |
| Dialogue ratio | 30-50% of text |
| Dialogue tags | "Said" dominant, minimal creative tags |
| -ly Adverbs | <105 per 10,000 words |
| Exclamation marks | <200 per 100,000 words (ideally far less) |
| Chapter length | 2,000-4,000 words (shorter = faster pace) |
| Dominant senses | Visual > Auditory > Touch > Smell > Taste |
| Language type | Concrete (nouns/verbs) over abstract (adj/adv) |
| Character verbs | Need, want, miss, love (2x frequency of non-bestsellers) |
| Topic concentration | 30-40% on 1-2 dominant themes |
| Emotional arc | Symmetrical rhythm of highs/lows |
| POV trend | Deep third person (2020s dominant) |
