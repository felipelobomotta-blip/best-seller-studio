# Bestseller Structural Patterns: Data-Driven Research

**Date:** 2026-03-13
**Purpose:** Structural patterns found in bestselling books via machine learning, neuroscience, and publishing industry data.
**Relevance:** "Protocolo Nao Encontrado" -- memoir, ~67,800 words.

## 1. THE BESTSELLER CODE (Archer & Jockers) -- Machine Learning on 20,000+ Novels

**Source:** Jodie Archer & Matthew L. Jockers, "The Bestseller Code" (2016). Five-year study. Algorithm trained on 5,000+ legally obtained fiction titles; broader corpus of 20,000+ novels analyzed. Algorithm predicts NYT bestseller status with **80-90% accuracy** (most sources cite 83%).

### 1.1 Topic Patterns (Most Predictive Feature)

- Bestsellers dedicate **30-40% of total topical content** to just 2-3 dominant topics.
- The **single most powerful topic** in bestselling fiction is **"human closeness"** -- not sex, not violence, not adventure.
- Fifty Shades of Grey was predicted as a bestseller NOT because of sexual content, but because of its **human closeness/intimacy** ratio.
- Topics that **correlate with bestsellers**: human connection, technology, work, family life, daily domestic life, marriage, death, taxes, funerals, guns, doctors, schools, presidents, newspapers, kids, moms, media.
- Topics that **anti-correlate with bestsellers**: explicit sex scenes, drug use, depictions of passionate love or desperate grief (as primary topics).
- Trivial but real finding: readers prefer novels with **dogs** over cats.

### 1.2 The Algorithm Identified 2,799 Features Strongly Associated with Bestsellers

These features span topic, style, plot, and sentiment. The algorithm weights them together but topic was the single most predictive category.

### 1.3 Style Findings

- Bestsellers use **short, simple sentences**.
- Heavy use of **contractions** (close to everyday vernacular).
- **Nouns and verbs** carry meaning -- bestselling authors do NOT overload sentences with adjectives and adverbs.
- Language closer to everyday speech wins over formal/literary prose.
- Readability and fluidity are more predictive than vocabulary sophistication.

### 1.4 Plot Shape: The "Bestseller Curve"

- All novels fall into one of **seven fundamental plot shapes** (echoing Vonnegut/Reagan research).
- The specific shape matters less than its **rhythm** -- bestsellers share a "remarkably consistent and symmetrical beat of emotional highs and lows."
- A **"W" shaped arc** occurs so frequently in bestsellers that Archer & Jockers named it "bestseller curves."
- The Da Vinci Code and Fifty Shades of Grey -- despite radically different content -- have **nearly identical plot graphs** when mapped by sentiment analysis.
- The pattern: a rhythmic alternation between positive and negative emotional valence, with regular interval turning points.

### 1.5 What the Algorithm Says Does NOT Matter

- Genre itself is not a strong predictor (bestsellers exist in every genre).
- Explicit content does not help.
- Literary "prestige" style does not predict sales.

## 2. SIX EMOTIONAL ARCS (Reagan et al., University of Vermont / MIT)

**Source:** Andrew Reagan et al., "The emotional arcs of stories are dominated by six basic shapes" (2016). Computational Story Lab, University of Vermont. Published in EPJ Data Science. Sentiment analysis of **1,700+ English fiction works** from Project Gutenberg (each downloaded 150+ times).

### 2.1 The Six Core Story Shapes

1. **Rags to Riches** -- steady rise (e.g., Alice's Adventures Underground)
2. **Tragedy / Riches to Rags** -- steady fall
3. **Man in a Hole** -- fall then rise
4. **Icarus** -- rise then fall
5. **Cinderella** -- rise, fall, rise
6. **Oedipus** -- fall, rise, fall

### 2.2 Most Popular / Most Downloaded

- The **three most successful** arcs (by download count) are: **Icarus, Oedipus, and "Man in a Hole."**
- The **single most popular** story structures are:
  - **Two sequential "Man in a Hole" arcs** (fall-rise-fall-rise)
  - **Cinderella arc followed by Tragedy** (rise-fall-rise-fall)
- Complex narratives that combine basic arcs in sequence outperform simple single-arc stories.

### 2.3 Implications

- The "W" shape from the Bestseller Code aligns with the **double "Man in a Hole"** pattern: down-up-down-up.
- Reader engagement peaks when emotional valence oscillates -- not when it stays flat or moves in only one direction.

## 3. STORY GRID (Shawn Coyne) -- Genre Conventions & Obligatory Scenes

**Source:** Shawn Coyne, "The Story Grid" (2015). 30+ years of editorial experience. Framework used by editors at major publishing houses.

### 3.1 Core Framework: Five Commandments of Storytelling

Every scene, sequence, act, and global story MUST contain:

1. **Inciting Incident** -- throws protagonist's life out of balance
2. **Progressive Complications** -- problems escalate (must get harder, not just multiply)
3. **Crisis** -- real dilemma with incompatible options and meaningful stakes
4. **Climax** -- active answer to the crisis question
5. **Resolution** -- consequence of the climactic choice

These five commandments apply at EVERY level: beat, scene, sequence, act, subplot, global story.

### 3.2 Foolscap Method (Global Structure)

Three macro sections:
- **Beginning Hook** (Act 1): Inciting incident, complication, crisis, climax, resolution
- **Middle Build** (Act 2): Same five, but escalated
- **Ending Payoff** (Act 3): Same five, but at maximum stakes

### 3.3 Genre-Specific Obligatory Scenes (Thriller Example)

- Inciting Crime indicative of a master Villain
- Multiple victims
- Speech in Praise of the Villain (reveals villain's cunning/brilliance)
- Protagonist becomes the Victim (crimes become personal)
- **Hero at the Mercy of the Villain** (core event -- protagonist unleashes their gift)
- **False Ending** (there must be two endings)
- Convention: A clock (time pressure on the protagonist)

### 3.4 Memoir in Story Grid

- Memoir uses the same five commandments and obligatory scene structure
- Common external genres for memoir: Love, Action, Thriller
- Memoir about abuse can fall into Crime, Horror, or Thriller arena
- The internal genre (character transformation) is usually the primary driver

### 3.5 Value Shifts

Every scene must have a **value shift** -- the protagonist's situation must change from positive to negative or vice versa. Scenes without value shifts are "dead scenes" and should be cut.

## 4. NEUROSCIENCE OF STORYTELLING (Lisa Cron + Paul Zak Research)

### 4.1 Lisa Cron ("Wired for Story" / "Story Genius")

- Stories evolved as a **survival mechanism** -- the brain uses narrative to simulate dangerous situations without real risk.
- The brain's hardwired desire to learn "what happens next" is the fundamental engine of engagement.
- When writers tap into this evolutionary purpose, it triggers a **dopamine rush** that commands attention.
- Story works from the **inside out**: external plot serves the internal transformation.

### 4.2 Paul Zak (Claremont Graduate University) -- Neurochemical Research

Three hormones drive story engagement:

| Hormone | Trigger | Effect |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **Cortisol** | Tension, conflict, danger | Focuses attention, enhances memory formation |
| **Oxytocin** | Character vulnerability, human connection, empathy moments | Creates emotional bonding with characters |
| **Dopamine** | Curiosity gaps, surprise, achievement, resolution | Reinforces attention, motivation, memory |

- **Key finding**: The most powerful oxytocin responses occur NOT when characters showcase strength, but when they reveal **humanity through struggle, doubt, and growth**.
- Stories that produce cortisol + oxytocin together cause people to **take action** after the story (donate money, change behavior).

### 4.3 Neural Coupling (fMRI Research)

- When listening to/reading a compelling story, listener brains **literally synchronize with the storyteller's brain** -- a phenomenon called "neural coupling" or "brain synchrony."
- This is narrative transportation: cognitive and emotional immersion that deeply engages the reader.

### 4.4 PNAS Study: Storytelling and Hormones

Published in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences:
- Storytelling **increases oxytocin** and positive emotions.
- Storytelling **decreases cortisol** and pain.
- The oxytocin increase had a **positive correlation with empathy** for characters.
- Heightened empathy motivated participants to offer money to strangers.

## 5. PUBLISHING INDUSTRY DATA: What Sells

### 5.1 Word Count by Genre (2024-2025 Data)

| Genre | Average Bestseller Word Count | Agent-Preferred Range |
|-------|-------------------------------|----------------------|
| Literary Fiction | ~98,000 | 80,000-95,000 (debut) |
| Mystery/Thriller/Suspense | ~91,000 | 70,000-100,000 |
| Romance | ~90,000 | 75,000-100,000 |
| Science Fiction | ~98,000 | 90,000-120,000 |
| Women's Fiction | ~93,000 | 80,000-100,000 |
| Memoir | ~68,000 | 60,000-90,000 |
| General Nonfiction | varies | 50,000-80,000 |

- Average NYT bestseller page count: **273 pages (~68,000 words)**.
- Bestseller average word count has **dropped 42% in the past 7 years** (Scribe Media study, 2017).
- Trend: books are getting shorter. Horror, Literary Fiction, and YA saw slight increases 2023-2024 but remain within standard ranges.

### 5.2 Chapter Length Patterns

- Average chapter: **8-15 pages (~2,000-4,000 words)**.
- Thrillers (Patterson, Brown): **2-5 pages per chapter (~1,000-2,000 words)**.
- Fiction general: **3,000-5,000 words per chapter**.
- Nonfiction: **4,000-6,000 words per chapter**.
- Patterson-style short chapters with cliffhanger endings = higher page-turn velocity.
- Some bestsellers have **50+ short chapters**, others have fewer than 10 long ones.

### 5.3 Reading Level / Readability

| Author | Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Ernest Hemingway (Old Man and the Sea) | **Grade 4** |
| J.K. Rowling (Harry Potter) | **Grade 5.7** |
| Jane Austen | **Grade 5+** |
| Stephen King | **Grade 6** |
| J.R.R. Tolkien | **Grade 6+** |

- Most popular novels are written at a **7th-grade reading level** (researcher George Klare).
- Average adult reads at Grade 9, but **recreationally prefers 2 grades below** their level.
- In the 1960s, average bestseller readability was **8th grade**. Now it is roughly **7th grade**.
- By 2014, **97% of bestselling books were below 7th-grade level**.
- No author examined -- not Hemingway, not Fitzgerald, not Tolstoy -- wrote above a **9th-grade level**.
- Takeaway: **accessibility and clarity beat complexity** in every measurable case.

### 5.4 POV Distribution

- **Third person** dominates Adult fiction bestseller lists.
- **First person** dominates YA and MG bestseller lists.
- **Memoir**: first person is the standard/expected POV.
- Both POVs produce bestsellers in all genres -- there is no single "correct" POV.
- **Deep POV** (close psychic distance) dominates romance; other genres use it variably.

### 5.5 Opening Patterns

- Analysis of 100 NYT bestsellers: favorite first lines balance **clarity and curiosity** equally.
- Common elements in successful openings: specific time references ("year"), memorable locations, contradiction or surprise.
- Effective first lines: punchy, single-sentence, authentic voice, raise an implicit question.
- In medias res (starting in the middle of action) is common in thrillers and literary fiction but is NOT the only successful approach.
- Key: the opening must create a **curiosity gap** that compels continued reading.

### 5.6 Ending Patterns

- **Closed/resolved endings** dominate mass-market bestsellers.
- Gestalt psychology: humans prefer complete forms. Narrative closure **reduces cognitive load** (Cognition journal study).
- Genre rules: romance REQUIRES a happy ending (HEA/HFN); detective fiction almost always solves the case; literary/mainstream fiction tolerates ambiguous endings.
- **Emotional satisfaction** is more important than plot resolution -- readers can handle open threads if the emotional arc resolves.
- Ambiguous endings are divisive: some readers love them, others feel cheated. Literary fiction readers are more tolerant.

## 6. PACING PATTERNS

### 6.1 Scene-Sequel Framework (Dwight Swain / Jack Bickham)

- **Scene** = unit of conflict: Goal -> Conflict -> Disaster
- **Sequel** = unit of transition: Reaction -> Dilemma -> Decision
- Fast pacing: **short sequels** (or skip them entirely)
- Slow pacing: **long sequels** (extended reflection, introspection)
- No specific "ideal ratio" has been empirically established, but the pattern in bestselling thrillers is heavily weighted toward scenes with minimal sequels.

### 6.2 Motivation-Reaction Units (MRUs)

- Created by Dwight V. Swain ("Techniques of the Selling Writer").
- Everything that happens = either a **cause (motivation)** or an **effect (reaction)**.
- Efficient prose alternates cleanly between external stimulus and character response.

### 6.3 Dialogue Percentage

- **Marlowe AI** (built by Jockers, co-author of The Bestseller Code): popular novels have dialogue between **25-35% of the book**.
- Too little dialogue = dense, slow. Too much = thin, lacks interiority.

### 6.4 Marlowe AI Structural Benchmarks

- Major turning point at approximately the **25% mark**.
- Major peak or valley at roughly **50%**, propelling the protagonist into final conflict.
- Successful writers **vary pace** throughout -- peaks (fast reading) and valleys (scene setting, reflection).

## 7. PLOT STRUCTURE DATA

### 7.1 Save the Cat Beat Sheet (Jessica Brody's Novel Analysis)

Percentages of total book length:

| Beat | Position |
|------|----------|
| Opening Image | 0-1% |
| Theme Stated | ~5% |
| Setup | 1-10% |
| Catalyst | ~10% |
| Debate | 10-20% |
| Break Into 2 | ~20% |
| B Story | ~22% |
| Fun and Games | 20-50% |
| Midpoint | ~50% |
| Bad Guys Close In | 50-75% |
| All Is Lost | ~75% |
| Dark Night of the Soul | 75-80% |
| Break Into 3 | ~80% |
| Finale | 80-99% |
| Final Image | 99-100% |

Jessica Brody has verified this structure in novels by Stephen King, J.K. Rowling, Agatha Christie, John Green, and Jojo Moyes.

### 7.2 Three-Act vs. Hero's Journey vs. Other

- Save the Cat nests within Three-Act structure but subdivides Act 2 into 15 beats.
- No empirical data exists on what percentage of bestsellers deviate from three-act structure.
- Practical consensus: nearly ALL commercially successful stories can be mapped to three-act structure, even if the author didn't consciously use it.
- The Hero's Journey (Campbell/Vogler) overlaps heavily with three-act and Save the Cat.
- Deviation from structure is possible but rare in commercial bestsellers; more common in literary fiction.

## 8. SYNTHESIS: PATTERNS THAT PREDICT BESTSELLERS

### The Data Converges on These Rules:

1. **Topic focus**: 30-40% of content on 2-3 dominant topics. Human closeness/intimacy is the #1 predictor.
2. **Emotional rhythm**: Regular oscillation between positive and negative emotional valence. The "W shape" (double Man-in-a-Hole) is the most common bestseller arc.
3. **Simple prose**: Grade 5-7 reading level. Short sentences. Contractions. Nouns + verbs over adjectives + adverbs.
4. **Structural beats**: Major turning points at ~25%, ~50%, ~75% marks. Five Commandments at every level.
5. **Dialogue**: 25-35% of total content.
6. **Word count**: Trending shorter. Memoir sweet spot: 60,000-80,000 words.
7. **Chapters**: Shorter is trending upward. Cliffhanger endings per chapter increase page-turn velocity.
8. **Opening**: Clarity + curiosity gap. One punchy sentence > paragraph of exposition.
9. **Ending**: Emotional resolution is mandatory. Plot ambiguity is tolerable only if emotional arc closes.
10. **Neurochemistry**: Conflict triggers cortisol (attention). Vulnerability triggers oxytocin (empathy). Curiosity gaps trigger dopamine (keep reading). All three must be present.

## 9. APPLICATION TO "PROTOCOLO NAO ENCONTRADO"

Current status: ~67,800 words (within memoir sweet spot of 60,000-90,000).

### Structural Checkpoints:

- [ ] Does 30-40% of content focus on 2-3 dominant topics? (human closeness, mental health struggle, identity)
- [ ] Does the emotional arc follow a "W" pattern or double Man-in-a-Hole? (fundo do poco -> recovery -> relapse/crisis -> transformation)
- [ ] Reading level: is prose at Grade 5-7? (check via Flesch-Kincaid)
- [ ] Dialogue percentage: is it between 25-35%?
- [ ] Major turning points at ~25% (~17,000 words), ~50% (~34,000 words), ~75% (~51,000 words)?
- [ ] Every chapter has a value shift (situation changes from positive to negative or vice versa)?
- [ ] Opening creates a curiosity gap in the first sentence?
- [ ] Ending provides emotional resolution even if some threads remain open?
- [ ] Five Commandments present at scene level? (Inciting Incident, Progressive Complications, Crisis, Climax, Resolution)
- [ ] Vulnerability > strength moments (oxytocin triggers)?
